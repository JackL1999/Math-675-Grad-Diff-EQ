$$x'(t) = 2x, \quad \quad y'(t) = ay$$
\begin{itemize}
\item[(a)] Use separation of variables to find the explicit solution for this system.
\begin{flalign*}
    \quad x'(t) &= 2x   & \quad y'(t) &= -ay &\\
    \quad \frac{dx}{dt} &= 2x &  \quad \frac{dy}{dt} &= ay \\
    \quad \int \frac{1}{x} dx &= \int 2 dt & \quad \int \frac{1}{y} dy &= \int a dt \\
    \quad \ln|x| &= 2t + c_1 & \quad \ln|y| &= at + c_1 \\
    \quad e^{\ln|x|} &= e^{2t + c_1} & \quad e^{\ln|y|} &= e^{at + c_1} \\
    \quad x &= e^{2t} e^{c_1} & \quad y &= e^{at} e^{c_1} \\
    \text{Solution: } x(t) &= c_1 e^{2t} & \text{Solution: } y(t) &= c_2 e^{at} &

\section*{Question 3:}
Let $a : \mathbb{R} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}$ be a continuous function. Consider the first order ODE
$$x' = a(t)x$$
\begin{itemize}
\item[(a)] Find a formula involving integrals for the solution of this system. \\
\begin{flalign*}
    \frac{dx}{dt} &= a(t)x(t) \\
    \int \frac{1}{x(t)} dx &= \int a(t) dt \\
    \ln|x(t)| &= \int a(t) dt + C \\
    x(t) &= e^{\int a(t) dt + C} \\ 
    x(t) &= Ce^{\int a(t) dt} \\ 
\end{flalign*}
Future knowledge: Hey look integrating factor!!
\item[(b)] Prove that your formula gives the general solution of this system. Hint: follow the sketch of proof done on
the first day of class

First, let $u(t)$ be any solution and let's differentiate $f(t) = u(t)e^{\int -a(t) dt}$
$$\frac{d}{dt} f(t) = \frac{d}{dt} u(t)e^{\int a(t) dt}$$
$$f'(t) = \frac{d}{dt}\left(u(t)\right)e^{\int -a(t) dt} + u(t)\left(\frac{d}{dt}e^{\int -a(t) dt}\right)$$
$$f'(t) = u'(t)$$
\\\\
Definition of The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus: \\ 
\textit{If $f$ is continuous on $[a,b]$ and $g(x) = \int_{a}^{x} f(t) dt$ then $g'(x) = f(x)$}
\begin{flalign*}
    x'(t) &= a(t)x(t) \\
    \frac{dx}{dt}\left(Ce^{\int a(t) dt}\right) &= a(t)\left(Ce^{\int a(t) dt}\right) \\
    \frac{dx}{dt}\left(\int a(t) dt\right)\left(Ce^{\int a(t) dt}\right) &= a(t)\left(Ce^{\int a(t) dt}\right) \\
    a(t)\left(Ce^{\int a(t) dt}\right) &= a(t)\left(Ce^{\int a(t) dt}\right) \\
    RHS &= LHS
\end{flalign*}
Thus, since both sides are equal, then $x(t) = Ce^{\int a(t) dt}$ is in fact the general solution.
Although, I do sense some explotation of the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus, since we differentiated an integral a bit too easily.
\end{itemize}
